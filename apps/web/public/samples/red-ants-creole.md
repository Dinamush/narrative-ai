## Creole Version

A sinister glow stretched across the horizon, painting ribbons of crimson and orange over the restless waters. On the old rotten wooden docks, two men waded beneath that wounded sky—one with a tattered cloak dancing in the cool breeze, the other with a stiff coat, unmoved by the wind.

The cloaked man spoke first. "Meh need money," he said, drawing a cigarette from a threadbare pocket and flicking a cheap, plastic lighter. Inhaling deeply, he fixed his gaze on the taller, broader figure beside him. "I know yuh doh like them people. What yuh want me do?"

The larger man answered without hesitation, his tone as flat as stone: "Kill them. Murda all o' them." He paused only to spit into the river's brown grave. "Them is fly—com-pe-tishan. Go in, do the wuk, and tek them thing. Ants, youse the man fuh the job."

At this, the cloaked man—Ant—brushed aside his garment. A faint gleam caught on a worn blade, strapped to his hip by ragged leather and cloth. "I gon need some up front," he demanded, voice low. "Ah hungry." He gestured to his mouth.

His uncle—because that's who this man was—let out a mocking chuckle. "Nephew, we run a fishing business. Tell July what yuh need, and she fix yuh up." His words rang hollow. 

He smirked, eyes half-lidded with cruel amusement. A faint red glint danced in his pupils—greed, or perhaps the reflection of some distant police light. Ant recalled rumors that some local police owed favors to his uncle; in this town, law and corruption often shared a meal.

Ant turned his gaze out to sea. "I cyant eat meat or thief," he confessed, as though it explained everything. "I get the 'knock'. I does fast and nothing could ketch meh." His voice was strangely calm, as if the prospect of killing weighed less on him than breaking this rule.

The uncle's expression twisted with disbelief and mirth. "You nah touch meat, but yuh bathe in blood?" He snorted. "Bai. Yuh built like a ants, but you get murda in yuh eye. Hungry fuh moh than food." He waved a dismissive hand. "I'll pay you when it's done. Now get out of my sight."

As the first hint of sunrise touched the horizon, Ant vanished into the scattering light, leaving only a wisp of smoke behind.

---

Some time later, hunger gnawed at Ant's insides. The memory of his uncle's words curdled in his stomach. Outside a small shop, under the dim glow of a hanging lamp, he approached an old woman who tended a modest stall. Her eyes were kind, reminding him of days long past.

"Marning aunty," he said, voice gentle. "help me nah, ma?" His lips were cracked and eyes red.

"Gyet from here boy!" Ant heard is his mind like a broken record. He braced, ready for the spit of scorn. 

Her gaze softened further, motherly concern washing over her features. "Wah yuh need, son?"

"Just something fuh eat," Ant replied, his voice strangely meek for a man soon to commit violence. "I can't stay. I have… wuk."

The old woman's brows knit with quiet sympathy. "This world not been kind to you, 'as it?" She knew the answer. Without waiting, she called, "Margaret, bring some bread. And a piece of meat—"

"Just bread, ma." Ant interrupted firmly. The old woman's eyes flickered with understanding, though it puzzled her.

The clangor of church bells echoed, masking the rumble in his stomach. She studied his face- marks, tears and punishment etched in skin... but something about him was familiar: the tattered white cloth, the look of a family she once prayed for.

Margaret emerged, footsteps light on vinyl flooring, carrying a small bag of bread. She handed it to Ant, who nodded, gratitude fading in his cruel visage. "Thank yuh," he said sincerely, before stepping back into the chill air.

A sudden crash—a glass bottle tipping from a shelf—stole the women's attention. By the time they looked up, Ant and his bread were gone, as though swept away by the night. Margaret leaned over the shop's edge, finding not a single trace of him. The two women exchanged baffled glances.

"That boy didn't used to go to we Church?" She asked.

"He resemble his mother." Margaret replied. 

---

Later, beneath a deepening grey sky and a soft drizzle, Ant knelt on a rooftop ledge. Below, rain patted gently against concrete and zinc top. He wiped away the grime from a small windowpane, peering into a narrow warehouse that stank of fish and salt. Four men worked inside, each of them ordinary enough—pulling crates, stacking gear, talking of returning home to their families. They had no idea they'd been marked.

From his vantage point, Ant watched them as if studying prey. His blade hand hovered, steady, cold. He had a job to do. He'd been paid—no, not yet. But he knew the arrangement. Kill, then eat. Kill, then survive another day in this cruel world.

"Help meh fuh move these boxes, John," one of the men said. "I can't wait fuh go home." Something about the men seemed familiar. 

"I got you, brother," replied the other. "Meh pickney waiting on me. Lebbe done quick."

Their voices filtered through the rain-smeared glass. Ant could taste the salt of the sea and the scent of bread lingering in his nostrils. His stomach rumbled softly. The men below were just doing their work. They had families, hopes, dreams. In another life, he might have bought fish from them, even smiled. But now, they were targets—unwanted competition. His uncle's words echoed in his mind: _Kill them, murda all of them._ He inhaled, letting the drizzle turn to rain. Red ants marching across his mind's eye—relentless, following orders... no room for mercy.

The church bell rang again, dissolving in the stomach acid-rain.

No time for doubts. He slipped his fingers under the skylight's edge, easing it open with care. The rain's steady tapping masked his descent. His boots touched down softly. One man half-turned, brow creased with sudden unease. Ant froze, becoming a shadow in the gloom.

"Lebbe hurry," said one of the workers. "We're almost done here."

"Yeah, I dead tired bai," said another. "Another hour and I'm **home**."

Home. The word set Ant's teeth on edge. He had no home. Only tasks, bargains, hunger. These men were luckier than he, and he resented their innocence as much as he pitied it.

With a deft step, he moved behind the first target. The blade struck fast, a muted gasp swallowed by wet air. Blood, warm and metallic, stained the smell of fish and salt. The others spun around, horror sparking in their eyes. This was not the place they knew—now it was a battlefield.

They rushed for weapons—hooks, a broken plank—anything.

Ant danced backward, forcing their frantic swings to clatter off metal frames and crates. He moved like a whisper, a red ant among a colony of unsuspecting prey. Another man fell, gurgling as he hit the ground.

"Stap—please!" cried a third, dropping his makeshift weapon. Ant's heart throbbed painfully. He never truly wanted this. The old woman's bread felt heavy in his pocket. But the deal was struck, and hunger had led him here. 

Ant swung brutally- the blade stuck in the man's shoulder. The frantic man grabbed ant, trying to pry the weapon from his bones. He cried out- a blood curdling terror that leaked marrow. "No please- I get chirren budday!" The man pleaded. He grabbed the blade by the edge. "Please budday!"

Ant fought the impulse to pull back and just run- to let this man see his children again- but there was no escaping this hell for either of them. 

With the other arm pinned under ant's knee, as if he was praying over him- he reached for a stone. 
He cracked the brick against Ant's face, dislocating the small man's jaw. He lie there, blade in his shoulder in agony. The man pleaded for a mercy Ant couldn't give.

Another swift slash, and the pleading man slumped. Silence, save for the rain.

He looked closer at the man's face. His blood ran cold. His own cousin's empty eyes stared at the ceiling, bloodshot and terrified. In his mind, sirens calling and a clock ticking. 

"I-I.." He uttered.

Ant stood amid the carnage, breathing heavily through his mask. He wiped the blade on a scrap of cloth and clicked his jaw back in. The job was done. His uncle would pay him. He would eat. He would live.


Yet, as he climbed back out under that bruised, crimson sky, no sense of triumph followed. Each drop of rain felt like judgment. Each distant wave a quiet accusation. Compassion had touched him through the old woman and Margaret's kindness, but he'd answered it with murder. He tried not to think about it. He tried to move on—survival, always survival.

A flash of lightning revealed empty streets and silent waves. Ant vanished into that silence, weighed down by bread and blood, hunger and regret, leaving only the soft resentment of rain behind.

Blackness hung cotton over the world. His swift steps rippled shallow across the rainwater's face, splashing resenting whispers.
Droplets hit his face, quiet condemnations of holy-water. At any moment he could melt. 

Razor-blades in his stomach, his shadow reached the docks, wood slimy and barnacled. 

The tall man stood there unbothered by the torrent- Like water wouldn't touch him.

"Uncle. It done. Ah done."

The man turned, like a barrel.

"Yuh good-fuh-nothing lil bai!" He started. 
"I ain' tell yuh KILL EVERYBODY!?" Uncle said, careless to who heard.
"Yuh left one man- an' he know yuh face. Dunce." Ant's mind flashed back- his cousin's gashed throat and greyed eyes.

"Yuh ain't getting nothing. Yuh shoulda check yuh wuk!"

"Get out of meh sight before di police come!" He waved a dismissive hand.

Ants hands and body were shaking. Something inside gnawed at him, more than hunger. It burned. Burned. The blisters on his hands ached.

The thought of his cousin's glazed-over eyes lingered in his mind. He was now just meat, like the fish in a stall. All for what? Now, nothing mattered. 

The road to hell was already paved. There was no going back now.
Mother's image appeared in his mind. 
Similar bandits had taken her. Missing. Orphaned.
None. Nothing. But coldness and the dead screams of a forsaken god. 
A god that never answered. No matter how much he called.
This man, now his god. 
It burned. And burned. Uncle. 
He killed them. All doubt was erased.

Ant snatched the still-warm steel, and swung with everything his essence could muster. He meant to rend this wretched beast in half.

"AAAARRRGH!" The blade cut cleanly across his face, throwing skin and blood. His features slipped, revealing white bone quickly reddened. 

The blade skipped off his face. Uncle grabbed Ant's arm.

"YOU!" Uncle cried out.

"MEH GUH KILL YUH!" He yelped.

Ant, powerless over the larger man let go of the blade. Uncle recoiled, trying to hold his face from sliding off.

Deep red and teeth fell onto the docks. Clicking like dice. 

The blade clangored into the water. Onlookers from boats and market stalls alike watched, jaws gaped and eyes wide.

The man, like a bull, charged ant. 

Ant retreated, moving like a shadow. He picked up glass bottles littered across the sticky market concrete. No dish could satiate his hunger now. 

The glass crashed with fierce screams, exploding hateful shrapnel. The cries of women filled the air as sparks flew. 

They stuck into his skin. Police sirens wailed close. Red and Blue lights. Uncle's charge fettered. 

Ant took the chance slip under a stall. His footsteps dinged the zinc rooftops, like hammers on a door.

Uncle retreated, bystanders gathered to his aide.

--

Days passed in silence. Ant hid in abandoned lots, slept under rusted awnings, ate scraps of stale bread until not a crumb remained. 

Relentless officers hunted him systematically, tightening a net he could almost feel on his skin. It was as if they hated him. He could hear the click of their boots and ring of their badges.

He grew weaker—hunger gnawing at him, body trembling at every sound. He refused to eat meat, clinging to that last shred of principle in a world that had corrupted everything else. But without real food, his strength ebbed.

"No.. I cyan't." He repeated.

One rainy afternoon, exhausted, cornered in an alleyway behind the market, he found a half-chewed piece of meat discarded near a gutter. His face turned just looking at it.


Hunger screamed. Survival whispered that no principle mattered anymore.

He hovered over it, stomach heaving at the smell. He imagined the old woman's kind eyes, Margaret's soft footsteps. They had offered bread freely, never asking for anything in return. What had he done with that kindness?

Tears mixed with rain as he lifted the spoiled meat to his lips. He gagged at the taste, forcing it down, feeling something break inside him.

He had killed for pay, betrayed family—if it could be called that—and now defiled his last moral boundary. He was hollow now. Nothing left.

He thought of his mother's face. As much as he could remember it. It was fuzzy- like mold over the bread

A shadow fell across him. Two officers, plainclothes but unmistakably hostile, hands on their guns. He tried to rise, knife shaking in his grip, but his legs wobbled. The meat offered no salvation.

They fired without warning. Gunshots cracked the air. The metal passed clean through his eye, coming clean through the other side of his head.

Ant stumbled, collapsing onto damp concrete. Blood seeped into rainwater, a final red stain against the world's indifference. His brains and regrets rested in the muck. 

The officers slipped away, task done, their footsteps fading into the rain. Ant's breathing grew shallow. He thought of bread and kindness, of principles and choices. His last vision faded, tears lost in the downpour, and let the darkness take him.

Tiny ants emerged, smelling the blood. They cover him in a blanket of red and sorrow.
